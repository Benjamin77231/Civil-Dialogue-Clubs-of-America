Our Story

How a single campus experiment became a national movement for civil dialogue in American high schools.

The Beginning

From one campus. To every school in America.

It started with a frustration that felt familiar: a college student who looked around his campus and saw two camps that refused to speak to each other. Not because they hated each other — but because there was no room designed for the conversation to happen.

So he built one. A club where the only rule that mattered was this: you show up ready to listen as hard as you argue. The name didn't matter. The party affiliation certainly didn't. What mattered was the table — and the willingness to sit at it with someone whose worldview looked nothing like yours.

Students came. They disagreed. They challenged each other. And — perhaps most surprisingly — they came back the following week. The format worked because it was built on respect, not consensus. The goal was never to agree. The goal was to understand.

The Expansion

High schools are where habits form.

College students are forming opinions. High school students are forming habits. The difference is significant — which is why the decision to expand to high schools wasn't just strategic. It was urgent.

Young people across America are coming of age in a media environment that rewards outrage and punishes nuance. They're learning about politics from the most polarizing corners of the internet, at the exact age when their civic instincts are being shaped.

CDCA exists to offer something different: a structured, student-led space where high schoolers can engage with the real complexity of political life before they've been fully sorted into teams.

Our Journey

Building toward a more civil America

Year One

The First Club

A single nonpartisan politics club launches at a university. First meetings draw a small, skeptical, diverse crowd — and it works better than anyone expected.

Year Two

The Model Takes Shape

Discussion formats are refined. The Facilitator's Guide is written. Recruitment strategies are tested. What started as an experiment becomes a replicable model.

Year Three

Expanding to High Schools

CDCA is formally founded with a mission to bring the college model to high schools across America. The Starter Kit is developed and the first high school clubs are launched.

Now

A Growing National Network

CDCA-affiliated clubs are active across multiple states. The Leader Training program formally launches for students who already run political or civics clubs.

The Goal

A Club in Every State

CDCA's near-term mission: a civil dialogue club in every state, and a generation of young leaders who know how to run them.

What We're For

Two missions. One purpose.

CDCA's work has two distinct tracks — and both are essential to the same larger goal.

🏫
Pillar One

Club Expansion

We provide high school students with everything they need to start a CDCA-affiliated nonpartisan politics club at their school. This means a formal Starter Kit, facilitation training, and ongoing support from the CDCA network.

A CDCA club isn't just another student organization. It's a commitment to a specific kind of space — one where the default is curiosity, not confrontation, and where the measure of a good meeting isn't agreement, but understanding.

🎓
Pillar Two

Leader Training

Thousands of students already lead political or civics clubs at their schools — but most were never taught how to facilitate a genuinely civil discussion. CDCA's training program addresses that gap directly.

We train student leaders in the specific skills that make hard conversations productive: how to create psychological safety, manage dominant voices, handle inflammatory comments, and build formats that surface real ideas rather than performative points.

Core Values

What every CDCA club stands for

⚖️

Strict Neutrality

CDCA as an organization holds no political positions and takes no policy stances. Our clubs are forums, not platforms. Every viewpoint is welcome at the table.

🗣️

Civil Discourse

We believe disagreement is not only healthy, but necessary. However, civility is the condition that makes meaningful disagreement possible. Without it, debate becomes performance and nothing gets learned.

🙋

Every Voice Matters

We believe that every student has a valuable perspective to contribute. Though we never pressure anyone to participate, we encourage all members to share their thoughts.

🎯

Student-Led

The best civic education is self-governed. CDCA never takes over a club — we equip students to run them. That means trusting young people with real responsibility, because that trust is itself a form of civic development.

🔍

Intellectual Honesty

We ask our members to engage with the strongest version of the arguments they disagree with. While strawmanning is easy, Steelmanning is where growth happens.

🌱

Long-Term Thinking

Meetings will not be perfect at first, and there will most certainly be challenges. However, we're trying to build a generation that knows how to talk across difference, and we're willing to do the slow work that requires.

Meet the Founder

The person behind CDCA

Founder & Executive Director, CDCA

I'm a student at The West End Secondary Public School in New York City with a deep frustration and a deeper belief — frustrated by how divided this country has become, and convinced that young people can actually do something about it.

It started small. I began creating informal political discussion spaces among my friends, many of whom had little interest in politics and even less awareness of what was happening around them. I wasn't trying to change their minds; instead, I was trying to open them up to different perspectives. Eventually, I realised that I could make an official forum for this kind of dialogue, so I created my school's first-ever politics club.

We built a community of young people across the political spectrum who came together, listened, and learned from each other. I thought, "if this could work here, why couldn't it work at other high schools?"

That question became the CDCA, a youth civic organization I founded to bring this model to high schools across America. The focus on high school is intentional. It's where political identities begin to form and where biases either take root or get questioned. If we can reach students at that moment, not to tell them what to think but to teach them how to think across difference, we have a real shot at changing the culture of political conversation in this country.
